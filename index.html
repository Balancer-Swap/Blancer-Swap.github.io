What Is Balancer and How It Differs From Uniswap and Curve

Balancer is a programmable AMM protocol that extends the standard constant product formula into a generalised constant weighted product — allowing pools with any number of tokens at any desired weight ratio, not just two tokens at 50/50.

This generalisation makes Balancer fundamentally more flexible than Uniswap (optimised for 50/50 volatile pairs) and more versatile than Curve (optimised for pegged stablecoin pairs). Balancer occupies the middle ground — handling everything from 2-token weighted pools to 8-token index-like portfolios, from stable pairs to yield-bearing boosted positions.

Balancer vs Uniswap

Uniswap forces 50/50 weighting — you always hold equal value of both tokens. Balancer lets you hold 80% ETH and 20% USDC in a pool, maintaining higher ETH exposure while earning fees. Uniswap V3 adds capital efficiency via concentration; Balancer adds weighting flexibility and multi-token pools.

Balancer vs Curve

Curve is optimised for pegged assets (USDC/USDT) using the StableSwap invariant — extremely low slippage near peg but not suitable for volatile assets. Balancer serves both volatile-weight pools and stable pools via its Stable Pool type, plus adds boosted pools with yield on idle capital and managed pools with dynamic weights.

Smart Order Routing: How Balancer Finds Best Execution for Every Swap

Balancer's Smart Order Router (SOR) is the algorithm that powers Balancer's swap interface — automatically finding the optimal trade path across all Balancer pools (and integrated external sources) to maximise output for traders.

What SOR does

For any swap, SOR evaluates every possible path: direct pool swaps, multi-hop routes (A→B→C), and split routes (50% through Pool 1 + 50% through Pool 2). It calculates the mathematically optimal split that maximises the output amount, executing across multiple pools in a single atomic transaction.

Multi-hop routingSplit executionSingle transaction

Why SOR benefits LPs

SOR routes volume to the most efficient pools — which means smaller, niche Balancer pools with unusual weightings can capture trading volume from large swaps that split across many pools. Even a 98/2 pool might receive part of a large ETH trade if it offers better partial execution than a direct single-pool route.

This drives fee income to Balancer LPs from aggregator-routed volume.

veBAL: Vote-Locking, Gauge Votes, Bribes, and Protocol Revenue Share

veBAL (vote-escrowed BAL) is Balancer's governance position — obtained by locking the BAL/WETH 80/20 Balancer Pool Token (BPT) for a period of up to 1 year. The longer the lock, the more veBAL received.

What veBAL controls

veBAL holders vote on which Balancer pools receive BAL gauge emissions each epoch. Directing emissions to a pool increases its LP APR, attracting more liquidity. This vote is extremely valuable — it's how protocols build deep liquidity on Balancer without continuously buying BAL.

veBAL revenue and bribes

veBAL holders earn: (1) a share of Balancer's protocol swap fees distributed as bb-a-USD (boosted stablecoin), (2) bribes from protocols paying veBAL voters to direct emissions to their pools via platforms like Hidden Hand. The bribe economy makes veBAL highly capital-efficient for long-term governance participants.

Protocol-Owned Liquidity: Why DeFi Projects Choose Balancer

Balancer has emerged as the preferred AMM for DeFi protocols managing their own treasury liquidity — particularly through the 80/20 pool model and Balancer's Liquidity Bootstrapping Pools (LBPs).

80/20 pools for protocols

A protocol that wants deep liquidity for its token can create an 80/20 TOKEN/ETH pool. The project provides 80% in their native token (which they hold anyway) and only 20% in ETH. This dramatically reduces the ETH capital required to bootstrap liquidity versus a 50/50 pool — Balancer itself uses this model for BAL/WETH liquidity.

Capital efficientNative token biasProtocol treasury

Liquidity Bootstrapping Pools (LBP)

LBPs are a Balancer-specific pool type for token launches — starting at a high weight (e.g. 90% new token) and gradually decreasing over days, creating natural downward price pressure that discourages bots and allows price discovery. Many major DeFi token launches have used Balancer LBPs as a fairer alternative to standard DEX launches.

How to Provide Liquidity on Balancer: Step-by-Step

  1. Navigate to the official Balancer app — go to app.balancer.fi (bookmarked URL). Verify the domain before connecting your wallet.
  2. Connect your wallet — Balancer is deployed on Ethereum mainnet, Arbitrum, Optimism, Polygon, and other networks. Select the correct network in your wallet before connecting.
  3. Browse pools — use the Pools section to find pools by TVL, APR, and pool type. Filter by your preferred token exposure and pool type (weighted, stable, boosted).
  4. Select a pool and review the composition — check the token weights, current APR (fees + BAL emissions), total TVL, and pool type before committing capital.
  5. Add liquidity — click "Add Liquidity" on your chosen pool. Balancer allows proportional or single-token joins (single-token join is more convenient but incurs a small price impact).
  6. Approve tokens and confirm — approve Balancer's vault contract to interact with your tokens, then confirm the liquidity transaction. You receive BPT (Balancer Pool Tokens) representing your share.
  7. Stake BPT in the gauge — for pools with gauge approval, stake your BPT in the corresponding gauge to earn BAL emissions on top of trading fees.
  8. Optional: lock BPT as veBAL — for the BAL/WETH 80/20 pool specifically, you can lock your BPT as veBAL to earn protocol fee revenue and participate in gauge voting.
Single-token joins: Balancer allows you to add liquidity using just one token even for multi-token pools. The protocol internally swaps to balance the pool, incurring a small price impact. For large additions, proportional joins (using all tokens in the pool's ratio) avoid this cost. Check the "price impact" indicator when using single-token joins for large amounts.

Best Practices for Balancer LPs and veBAL Holders

For liquidity providers

  • Match pool type to your asset strategy — use weighted pools for directional token exposure, stable pools for stablecoin pairs, and boosted pools when you want additional yield on stable capital without extra management.
  • Stake BPT in the gauge immediately after adding liquidity — unstaked BPT earns only trading fees. The BAL emission component (often 50–80% of total APR) requires gauge staking. Don't leave BPT unstaked.
  • For large single-token joins, check price impact — use proportional joins (multi-token at pool ratio) for large amounts to avoid self-inflicted slippage from the internal rebalancing swap.
  • Monitor boosted pool underlying protocol health — if Aave or Yearn experiences issues, boosted pool values are directly affected. Diversify across pool types for larger LP positions.

For veBAL holders

  • Vote every epoch on Hidden Hand — unclaimed bribe epochs are forfeited. Set a recurring reminder for every 2-week governance period to vote and claim your share of bribe rewards.
  • Relock before expiry to maintain voting power — veBAL decays linearly. Relocking at max duration regularly maximises both your boost and bribe earnings.
  • Consider Aura Finance for delegated veBAL — Aura aggregates veBAL similarly to how Convex aggregates veCRV, offering enhanced BAL boost and bribe capture without requiring direct pool participation. Evaluate Aura vs direct veBAL based on your position size and management preference.

Troubleshooting Balancer: Swap Failures, LP Issues, and veBAL Locks

"My swap failed on Balancer"

  • Check the failed transaction on Etherscan for the revert reason. Common Balancer-specific errors: SWAP_LIMIT (slippage exceeded — price moved beyond your tolerance), BAL#507 (insufficient pool liquidity for the requested trade size).
  • For large swaps, try using a smaller amount or increasing slippage. Alternatively, use a DEX aggregator (1inch, Paraswap) which may find a better-split route using Balancer's SOR alongside other protocols.

"My BPT isn't showing after adding liquidity"

  • Verify the transaction on Etherscan — if it succeeded, the BPT is in your wallet. Add the BPT contract address manually to your wallet's token list if it doesn't appear automatically.
  • If you staked BPT in the gauge immediately, it will show in the Balancer gauge dashboard rather than your wallet balance. Check your staked positions in the Balancer app's portfolio view.

"I can't unlock my veBAL early"

  • veBAL locks are absolute — the locked BPT cannot be withdrawn before the expiry date under any circumstances. There is no emergency exit. Plan capital allocation around this constraint before locking.
  • If your veBAL has expired but still shows locked, initiate the withdrawal via the veBAL section of the Balancer app — expired locks must be manually withdrawn, they don't automatically return to your wallet.
Balancer's Vault is the canonical source: All Balancer pool assets are held in a single central Vault contract. For any LP balance or swap verification, use Etherscan with your wallet address and the Balancer Vault contract address to confirm the on-chain state definitively.

Balancer: Frequently Asked Questions

Balancer is a programmable AMM that extends the constant product formula to support pools with any token weighting (not just 50/50), up to 8 tokens per pool, and multiple pool types (weighted, stable, boosted, managed). This means an LP can hold 80% ETH and 20% USDC in a pool — maintaining directional bias — while still earning fees from all trades. Uniswap forces 50/50 and only supports 2-token pools. Balancer's flexibility makes it the preferred AMM for protocol-owned liquidity and DeFi index strategies.

An 80/20 pool holds 80% of one token and 20% of another — you're providing liquidity while maintaining a heavily skewed exposure. Key benefit: significantly lower impermanent loss than a 50/50 pool, because your exposure is closer to simply holding the dominant token. Protocols use 80/20 pools (TOKEN/ETH) to create deep liquidity while only needing 20% ETH capital — the rest is their own native token. Balancer's own BAL/WETH 80/20 pool is the canonical example, and locking its BPT gives you veBAL.

Boosted pools deploy the idle portion of pool liquidity (the capital not currently needed for swaps) to external yield protocols like Aave, earning lending interest while the capital waits for trades. An LP in a boosted stablecoin pool earns both Balancer trading fees (from swaps) and Aave lending yield (from idle capital) simultaneously. This makes boosted pools significantly more capital-efficient than standard AMM pools where idle liquidity earns nothing.

veBAL (vote-escrowed BAL) is obtained by locking the BAL/WETH 80/20 Balancer Pool Token (BPT) for up to 1 year. First, become an LP in the BAL/WETH 80/20 pool to receive BPT. Then lock that BPT via the veBAL interface — the longer your lock, the more veBAL you receive. veBAL holders earn 75% of protocol swap fees (in bb-a-USD), vote on gauge weights to direct BAL emissions, earn LP boosts (up to 2.5×), and receive bribes from protocols competing for gauge votes.

The Smart Order Router (SOR) is Balancer's algorithm that finds the optimal swap path — including direct swaps, multi-hop routes (A→B→C), and split execution across multiple pools simultaneously in one transaction. For traders, this means best possible output for any swap. For LPs, this means even niche pools with unusual weightings can capture partial trade flow from large trades that benefit from splitting. SOR is also integrated into aggregators like 1inch, routing external volume to Balancer pools.

Aura Finance is to Balancer what Convex Finance is to Curve — it's a veBAL aggregator. Aura pools BAL from users, converts it to veBAL en masse, and shares the governance power and rewards back to depositors. By using Aura, smaller BAL holders access near-maximum veBAL boost without locking their own BPT, while Aura handles governance on their behalf. AURA tokens accrue the value of veBAL control. For large BAL positions, direct veBAL locking gives more control; for smaller positions, Aura offers better efficiency.

An LBP is a Balancer managed pool used for token launches where the new token starts at a very high weight (e.g. 90%) and the weight gradually decreases over the launch period (e.g. 3–7 days). This natural weight shift creates downward price pressure, discouraging bots from front-running the launch and allowing organic price discovery. Buyers have an incentive to wait for the price to fall rather than front-run. Many DeFi protocols (Perpetual Protocol, Radicle, others) have used LBPs as a fairer alternative to standard IDO launches.

Impermanent loss (IL) on a 50/50 Uniswap pool is the standard reference. On a Balancer 80/20 pool, IL is significantly lower for the same price movement — because your exposure is already heavily skewed toward the dominant asset, so price divergence has less rebalancing impact. For example, if ETH 2× from your entry point, an 80/20 ETH/USDC Balancer pool incurs roughly 0.6% IL versus roughly 5.7% IL for a 50/50 Uniswap pool. The tradeoff: lower fees because less arbitrage occurs in asymmetric pools.
